Origin of Budapest:
Budapest was formed in 1873, with the merging of Buda, Pest and Obuda.
Hungarian forint:
Although member of the EU, Hungary is not using the euro. In Feb 2013, one euro was worth 282HUF (or 282ft). Many money-changers along Vaci ut
Oldest metro:
Opened in 1896, Line 1 of the Budapest underground is the oldest metro in continental Europe and the second oldest in the world (after the London Underground).
Free travel for EU seniors:
If you are over 65 years old and are from an EU country you don't have to pay to use the public transport in Budapest. Just show them your ID or passport! (Source: http://www.bkv.hu/en/faq/faq)

Cyber cafe:
You are going to have a hard time finding a cyber cafe in Budapest and the few whose addresses are given in some guide books just don't seem to exist anymore. Go figure! About the only one I came across after much searching is at number 42 of the busy street called Vaci utca. There are a few computers connected to internet on the top floor of this souvenir shop. It charges a flat rate of 300 forints for every 15 minutes.
Map of Budapest city:
Click to open map in new window. Souvenirs to bring home:
You don't have to go far to look for souvenirs to bring home. At the Central Market you will be able to find them all.
A typical gift from Hungary is a liquor drink containing 40% alcohol called unicum. This is what Hungary is famous for. It is actually a bitter drink made from 40 plants and can easily be distinguished as it is in a round bottle with the red cross symbol as its distinguishing mark.
Another souvenir to bring home is a set of paprika samples for cooking purposes.

What makes Budapest so different from all other European cities are its thermal baths. The highlight of your stay in Budapest could be a visit to the Szechenyi baths. And if you suffer from arthritis or back pains it adds more reason to be in there. Together with the Gellert bath, they are the biggest (and most expensive) of the numerous thermal baths After all it's not for nothing that it's called the City of Spas. So a thermal bath is a not-to-be-missed experience when you are in Budapest. It can even be the highlight of your stay there especially if you suffer from arthritis or other . When in Budapest do as the locals do, that is, go to the thermal baths. In fact there are not that many places to sightsee here when compared with cities like Athens, Rome or Barcelona but the thermal bath experience here is something that you will hardly find in other big European cities. More reason if you suffer from arthritis! So bring your towel, soap and slippers, put on a bathing robe like the Romans did in antiquity and go to at least the two most-renowned bathing houses in Budapest, namely the Gellerfurdo and the Szechenyi so do take advantage of them when you are here in Hungary. In fact they open as early as 6am and remains open till about 19h00 every day of the week. I doubt if you will be able to play a game of floating chess as the locals do though, but if you manage to do that, you can really say "Been there, done that"!

AIRPORT:
The airport is called Budapest Liszt Ferenc International Airport (it was formerly called Budapest Ferihegy International Airport. (http://bud.hu/english)
The best way from airport to the city using public transport is to take Bus 200E which connects Budapest Liszt Ferenc International Airport Terminal 2 to Kobanya-Kispest metro station (L3). From here change to M1 or M2 at Deak Ferenc ter. Bus 200E runs every 8-12 minutes in the daytime and every 15-20 minutes in the evening.

Vasarcsarnok (covered main central market). The capital's largest indoor market can be found at the head of the Szabadsag Bridge on the Pest side, on Fovam square (Fovam ter). After it was built in 1897, along with five others of its kind (at Rakoczi square, Klauzal square, Hunyadi square, Battyany square and Hold street) all built in similar style, were all opened on the same day to the public. The largest and most modern of them all however, is this one, the Grand Market Hall at Fovam square.
