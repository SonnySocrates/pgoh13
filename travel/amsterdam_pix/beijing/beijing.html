  VISITING BEIJING (through pictures)  


Hordes of tourists throng the Palace Museum (also known as the Forbidden City) daily to walk in the footsteps of the emperors of China's past dynasties.

On the grounds of the Imperial Gardens, which is at the end of the Palace Museum. The cypress trees have really stood the test of time.



The entrance to the walls of Tian An Men, under Mao Zedong's ever watchful eyes.

Two policemen keeping an eye (or rather four eyes) over the whole courtyard of Tian An Men.



A big crowd is always present to watch the unfurling of the flag at Tian An Men Square every evening at sunset.

The Sun Yat-Sen Park is just next to Tian An Men. Good place to rest your legs after your visit to the Forbidden City.



The Summer Palace (Yiheyuan), popular retreat of emperors and empresses from the summer heat, is now open to anyone who is willing to pay the 15 RMB admission fee.

There you will also come across the Long Corridor which stretches over 728 metres and is covered with 14,000 Chinese paintings in its beams and crossbeams.



This pedestrian walk is near the Qianmen underground station and is well worth a stroll though the first street to the right is full of shops that only have tourists in mind.

The same pedestrian walk is even more alive with its colourful illuminations at night.



A favourite pastime among the younger generation is singing at karaokes. Private rooms are easily available for such purposes.

One of the 15 or so temples found in the compound of the Yonghegong Lama Temple. The more fervent among the devotees burn incense in one after another of the temples.



You might have heard of the hutongs of Beijing. It simply means a lane or an alley. Whole neighbourhoods in Beijing are made up of hutongs crisscrossing each other in a labyrinth-like fashion.

Many have braved it to Tower 8, which is the highest point of the Badaling Great Wall. The real difficulty is not in the distance but in the steepness and in the unevenness of the steps.



This calligraphy expert at the Summer Palace uses not one, but two brushes, to write two words, one from each hand, at the same time.

Itinerant roadside hawkers such as this one are a common feature of Beijing streets. The snacks are prepared right in front of your eyes and are ideal for a quick bite.



Wangfujing Road is not only a very busy business area but a "pedestrians only" stretch of road popular with strollers and food hunters alike for its gourmets' street.

This hawker takes a devilish delight in offering scorpions and sea-horses to passers-by at the gourmets' street off Wangfujing Road. But there were hardly any takers.



The Beijing 2008 Olympic Games are over but visitors to the city continue to flock to the Olympic Park and its neighbouring Water Cube.

A two-storey bicycle park at the Deshengmen underground station for workers who collect them after returning from work by the tube.
