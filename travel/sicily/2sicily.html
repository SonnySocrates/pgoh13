Visiting Sicily in Italy

After Rome and Venise you might want to visit the island of Sicily, a name that evokes the mafia and Godfather films, despite the fact that it has lost its notoriety of former days for a long time now. Some low-cost airlines such as EasyJet have direct flights to Catania, the second biggest city in Sicily after Palermo. If that is the case you can easily make Catania your base to go to Syracus (Siracusa) for a day visit and return to Catania for the night and then take the coach for Palermo the next day.
There are two things you have to keep in mind when you are in Sicily. You cannot buy tickets on most buses and if you don't have one when you board the bus they'll probably drop you at the next bus-stop. So get your bus tickets from the "tabacchi" (tobacconists) before boarding your bus.
The second thing to bear in mind is that after deciding what you want to order in a pastry shop you have to pay first and get a scontrino (receipt) and only with that can you go to collect your order. In other words it's a case of Pay first, eat later.

Palermo is actually Sicily's capital city. If you come by coach to Palermo from Catania, the bus will stop next to the Central Railway Station of Palermo. The coach from Catania to Palermo is run by the SAIS company. Make sure you don't miss the coach as it is some 100 metres away from the booking office on the opposing side of the road. The trip takes 2 hours and 40 minutes and a ticket costs 15.20 euros. Although the return ticket costs 24.20 euros they will refuse to sell one if the return trip is not on the same day. There is a bus every hour or every 1 1/2 hours, depending on the time of the day.
It's easy to make a quick tour of Palermo if you have only a day. From the Central Station cross the big square and walk down Via Roma for less than a kilometre until its intersection with Via Vittorio Emanuele. Turn right if you want to see the Vucciria market and Piazza San Domenica else turn left for the Pretoria Fountain with its unique nude statues. If you continue walking straight ahead you will notice the spectacular Quattro Canti (meaning four corners) at its intersection with Via Maqueda in the Piazza Vigliena. In fact for a minute I really couldn't tell if I am in the middle of a busy intersection or in a museum, such is the magnificence of the four facades on four different and opposing buildings. If you continue walking you will notice Palermo's famous cathedral to your right. Leaving the Cathedral to continue walking along the same road you will not fail to notice the Porta Nuova at the very end of the road and to its left the Palazzo Reale.

While in Palermo don't miss the nearly 1km-long Mondello beach about 10kms away. It is really second to none. To go there take Bus 101 from near the Central Railway Station (Stazione Centrale) or at one of its stops along the Via Roma. Get down at Politeama and wait at the same busstop for Bus No. 806 which will take you all the way to Mondello beach (the bus actually ends there).

A single bus ticket costing 1.40 euros will allow you to take both buses (in fact the ticket is valid for 90 minutes). The promenade along the Mondello beach is a popular spot for joggers. After filling your lungs with sea air you might want to stop for a snack at Scimone (near the Alle Terraze restaurant), and try out typical Sicilian pastries such as the arancini (fried rice balls) and the cannoli, a tube-shaped pastry dessert (see photo).

Syracus  

There is a direct coach from Catania to Syracus. The trip takes about 1 1/2 hours and costs 7 euros. Once you arrive at the coach terminus turn backwards and walk straight on till you arrive at the crossroads. There follow the directions pointing to Ortygia which is the central point of interest. Keep walking straight on for about a km till you come to a bridge. Cross the bridge on the left rather than that on the right which is more meant for motorists. Once across the bridge you will see the remains of what was formerly the Apollo Temple. Continue walking forward until you reach the Piazza Archimede (see photo). From here a short walk will take you to Syracus's Duomo in the Piazza del Duomo.

Catania  

If Catania should be your first stop in Sicily be prepared for some unpleasant surprises. The public transport especially is quite chaotic. There are five or six different bus terminals and they are quite far from each other so one can easily get lost going from one terminal to another in search of a bus.
Even the Tourism Office at 172, Via Vittorio Emanuele II could not give me much information about the various bus routes. The best suggestion I can offer if you should pass a night in Catania before exploring the rest of Sicily is to stay in a hotel near the Piazza Duomo. The celebrated cathedral is found here and the principal shopping road, la Via Etnea, starts from here. Besides it is within walking distance of the central railway station and the booking offices of long-distance buses going to Palermo and Syracuse.
While I don't usually recommend hotels to stay in I'll make an exception for Catania as it is not easy to find good budget hotels here. As I personally stayed in the Hotel Biscari at 7, Via Anzalone, I can personally recommend it. The drawback is that you have to climb up two floors to get to the hotel so it is not for those who have difficulty in climbing stairs. If that is not a problem then it is a good place to stay in Catania as it is very well situated, being very near to the Piazza Duomo, the fish market, the Teatro Romano, the main shopping thoroughfare at Via Etnea and the central railway station and long-distance bus terminuses. Summer prices are a bit higher but for the rest of the year its rates are as follows: 30€ (for one person, TV, shower and breakfast included) and 50€ (for two persons).
Apart from the Etna volcanic mountain another must-visit place in Catania is Taormina, which is endowed with a mountain as well as a sea and is as lively at night as during the daytime, especially in summer.
If you are not scared of crowds the best time to come to Catania is during the first week of February (3rd to 5th Feb?) when the annual procession in honour of Saint Agatha, the patron saint of Catania, takes place.
But any time of the year is good for a visit to Mount Etna, though it is even more fun in winter when its summit (accessible by cable car) is sometimes covered with snow, making you feel that you are at a ski resort. However there is only one bus service a day to and from the mountain from near the Catania central train station. The AST company bus leaves at 08h15 daily for Etna and the return trip is at 16h30. The trip either way takes two hours with a short stop at Nicolosi, which is the town nearest to the mountain. The return ticket costs 10 euros(to check). I understand though that there is another trip from Catania at 11h20 during the three months from 15 June to 15 September. You can check from their website here (it's in Italian).
