Tau eu bak - an exquisite Chinese dish



This dish - yet another version of the famous tau eu bak also spelt as tau yu bak (braised pork in soy sauce) - is simply delicious and I bet anyone can cook it successfully provided he is able to obtain the necessary ingredients. Oh, by the way, this dish is not often found in Chinese restaurants so if you are hung on this dish you will have to cook it yourself!
I know how frightening many recipe books can be, with a list of more than a dozen ingredients needed to make just a single dish - enough to scare the daylights out of you.
Being such a person myself (I mean, someone who is easily put off by complicating recipes) I will include here only what is absolutely necessary so that even a novice will be able to give it a try.
However, although actual preparation time is at a minimum, it needs an hour to simmer, so prepare it well ahead of mealtime. Cooked this way, the fatty part of the pork (including the normally thick skin) is really heavenly.


The finished product. You might prefer to cut the pork belly into bigger pieces.

Basic recipe for pork belly in Chinese cooking wine



The 4 principal ingredients: pork belly, spring onions, ginger, Chinese cooking wine (shaoxing wine)

Ingredients (for 4-6 persons):

  • 600gm to 1kg of pork belly in big slices
  • One bundle of spring onion leaves (about 100gm)
  • One big piece of ginger
  • Chinese cooking wine (shaoxing wine)
  • loose brown sugar (else use white sugar)
  • soy sauce
  • (optional) You can add hard-boiled eggs, tofu cut into small pieces and potato cubes to make it a unique and filling dish that can be eaten with just white rice for lunch or dinner.
  • (optional) boil bok choy, a popular Chinese vegie and add to the sauce
Preparation (step-by-step):
1. Put water in a pot to boil. When the water starts boiling put in the large pieces of pork belly. (I find it easier to cut into pieces after the pork is boiled. Some people prefer to cut the pork into small pieces before putting them in boiling water).
2. Leave them in the boiling water for 5 minutes, then throw away the scum and water.
3. Meanwhile cut the spring onions (and the leaves) into 4-5 cm lengths and the ginger in big, flat slices. The ginger in big slices is for those who find ginger too strong to eat so they can be removed easily. I personally like to eat ginger so I mince them up tiny so they become part of the sauce. (Though not indispensable, I often add minced garlic as well).
4. Put in a bowl 6 tablespoonfuls of soy sauce (you can use light or dark soy sauce, or a combination of both, according to your preference) and about the same amount of Chinese cooking wine.
5. By now the large pieces of pork belly have cooled so it is easy to cut them up into thin slices (some might prefer slightly thicker slices).
6. Now put the cut pork pieces into the bowl and leave them to marinate.
7. In the meantime heat enough oil in a saucepan or a "creuset" (the oil should just cover the surface). When the oil becomes hot, throw in the ginger slices and the chives/spring onion and stir for 1 or 2 minutes. Then add 5 or 6 tablespoonfuls of loose brown sugar (if not use white sugar) and stir again. The beautiful part of this dish is that it is very flexible which means that even if you put in too much or too little of soy sauce, cooking wine, sugar, ginger or spring onion it will still be good so don't be afraid to experiment.
8. Add in immediately the pieces of pork belly (with all the sauce in the bowl) and stir-fry in strong heat till it changes colour.
9. Cover the saucepan completely, reduce the heat to moderate and let it simmer for one hour (and not a minute less, if you want the pork to be really succulent).
10. If you have the optional items such as hard-boiled eggs, tofu, potato cubes or vegie add them in halfway. And that's it, your work is done and you have one hour to do something else (though from time to time you should open the lid to give it a stir).
11. Serve hot with white rice.

Note: How much or how little of each ingredient to use is really a matter of taste and comes with practice and through trial and error.
When you buy a bottle of Chinese cooking wine (i.e. if you happen to live in one of those big cities with a Chinatown) look at the price. It should cost around two dollars. If it costs four or five times more it is probably Chinese wine for drinking (though it can be used for cooking as well - but why pay through your nose for it).
Hard-boiled eggs are often added to the saucepan, turning it into a dark brown colour. Besides the eggs, I sometimes add small pieces of tofu and potato cubes to it, making the dish more complete, especially if you can't eat too much of the pork belly. The good thing about this dish is that it can easily be kept over for the next day or two.
Postscript: A "bigger" version of this is called "dong1 po1 rou4", which comes from Hangzhou, about 160km from Shanghai. Here the pork belly is cut into "chunks" of square pieces of 6-7cm. In such a case the meat has to be left simmering for even longer.


(30 July 2016) Tried the "dong1 po1 rou4" today. Put 600gm of pork belly (in 4-5cm thick slices) into a pot of boiling water for 5 minutes. In the meantime mince the ginger and cut a bunch of six spring onions into 4cm pieces. Remove the pork belly from the pot to allow it to cool. Now put into a big bowl 6 tablespoonfuls of soy sauce (light or dark as you wish) and the same amount of Chinese cooking wine. Heat up some oil in the saucepan. When the pork belly has cooled cut it into 5 big chunks and put them into the big bowl to marinate. By now the oil should be boiling. Put in the ginger and spring onion and fry-stir for a minute or two then add 6 tablespoonfuls of brown sugar to the saucepan and continue stirring. Put in the marinated pork together with the sauce and continued frying at great heat, stirring all the time. Finally lower the heat and put on the lid for it to stew for an hour.
